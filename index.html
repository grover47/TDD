    Grand Circus aims to fuel Detroit's improbable dream

    November 3, 2013

    Is Detroit really poised to be a technology hot spot, with hundreds of young brainiacs creating software apps in scores of start-up companies sprouting in and around Dan Gilbert?s downtown buildings?

    Or is this merely a passing pipe dream, predicated on the romantic notion of impoverished Detroit as the ultimate urban underdog story, rising from chump to champ like Rocky Balboa of movie fame?

    For clues to how this story may unfold, keep an eye on Grand Circus, a new, small but very ambitious technology training firm that aims to serve as catalyst and glue for Detroit?s emerging tech scene as it spring boards off Google?s recent designation of Detroit as one of seven North American ?tech hubs.?

    Last week, I spent two hours on-site at the Broderick Tower, home of the firm at Woodward Avenue and Grand Circus Park (thus the company name) with its two cofounders.

    ■ CEO Damien Rocchi. He?s a 41-year-old Australian, Wharton School MBA holder, and a former McKinsey consultant. He?s married to a Detroit native.

    ■ And Bradley Hoos, 35, chief operating officer, a Grand Rapids native with engineering degrees from Michigan State and Iowa and an MBA from University of Chicago, who recently moved back to Michigan after seven years of consulting and building businesses in Chicago.

    Why, I asked them, would smart young talent seeking to start, or work for, a technology firm decide to locate in Detroit? Why not Ann Arbor, or Austin, Texas?

    ?In other places, you just don?t make the same amount of noise as you can in Detroit,? said Rocchi, calling the tech scene here more collaborative, with better direct access to key investors than other cities.

    ?In Detroit, the reality is ahead of the perception,? said Hoos, insisting there are many talented people coming out of Michigan schools who want the urban experience of working and living in Detroit.

    At present, Grand Circus is a company of six full-time employees, just one month into its primary mission of training people to fill what everyone agrees is a shortage of tech talent in specific areas such as software development ? a critical need if the fledgling start-ups now sprouting in Detroit are to scale up and become the next Groupon or Twitter.

    Located in 15,000 square feet of still unfinished space on floors 2-4 of the 34-story Broderick Tower, Grand Circus has received $1.5 million in funding from Detroit Venture Partners, plus additional support from Invest Detroit and the Downtown Detroit Partnership.

    Rocchi and Hoos said they expect to be cash-flow positive and generate $2 million in revenue next year, from a combination of tech training classes, event hosting and leasing co-working space to other start-ups and tech-related outfits such as Oakland County-based Automation Alley, which established a Detroit office in September.

    Classes offered range from 10-week courses on topics including building Android apps or dynamic websites to multi-day workshops on learning to code, or 3-hour beginner seminars on writing a business plan or introduction to the Internet. Grand Circus has 20 instructors working as contractors, about 60 students enrolled in 10-week courses and more than 150 signed up for seminars in workshops.

    The idea for creating Grand Circus sprung from a group of key players in funding the emerging technology hub in Detroit, including Josh Linkner, managing partner of DVP, which is backed by Quicken Loans Chairman Dan Gilbert and Brian Hermelin, chairman of Rockbridge Growth Equity.

    ?For Detroit to expand its vibrant tech scene, closing the talent gap is mission critical. Grand Circus helps bridge this gap by providing practical training for the skills in the highest demand,? Linkner said. Growing firms need people with specific tech skills that are in short supply, he noted, while thousands of talented people in the region are looking for new opportunities but need to learn new skills.

    When I asked Rocchi why he is confident Detroit will succeed when scores of other major cities also aspire to be hubs for high-tech entrepreneurs, he replied, ?Because Google thinks so.?

    A month ago, Grand Circus hosted an event to announce that Google for Entrepreneurs, an umbrella effort by Google to support entrepreneurs, has adopted Detroit as one of seven ?tech hubs? in North America ? along with others in Chicago, Nashville, Minneapolis, Denver, Durham, N.C., and Waterloo, Ontario.

    Exactly what that means is still fuzzy, but Rocchi and Hoos attended a recent Google for Entrepreneurs conference in California and returned brimming with optimism about the partnership.

    ?They want to change the world by spawning this army of entrepreneurs,? Rocchi said. ?They speak with their pocketbooks. They could have chosen other markets.?

    True enough. The same can be said of Detroit?s Gilbert, who is pouring real money behind downtown buildings and a growing cluster of start-up companies.

    If you?ve got an underdog story as big and as daunting as Rocky Balboa?s in mind, it?s good to start off with a Google and a Gilbert in your corner.
